# Research Writing Checklists

## Whole Paper

- Is the target genre clear: research article, review, letter, report, thesis chapter, grant proposal, or conference abstract?
- Is the likely reader clear, including interdisciplinary or non-native English readers?
- Does each section perform a distinct function?
- Does every paragraph have one main function?
- Does each sentence either advance information, explain function, or guide the reader?
- Are technical terms used consistently?
- Are claims calibrated to the evidence?
- Are figures and tables placed where the reader needs them?
- Are transitions explicit enough?
- Are the title and abstract accurate previews of the paper?

## Introduction

- The opening establishes a recognizable research territory.
- Background information is selective, not a mini-review.
- The gap, problem, or unresolved need is explicit.
- The gap logically motivates this study.
- The aim or research question is stated clearly.
- Novelty or contribution is specific and not inflated.
- The final sentences prepare the reader for the rest of the paper.

## Methods

- The study design or framework is clear.
- Materials, data, participants, instruments, or systems are identifiable.
- Procedure order is logical and reproducible.
- Key parameters, thresholds, and exclusions are stated.
- Non-obvious choices are justified.
- Analysis methods match the research questions.
- Ethics, approvals, software, data availability, or validation are included where relevant.

## Results

- Each results paragraph starts by orienting the reader.
- Findings are reported before broad interpretation.
- Quantities, uncertainty, and comparisons are clear.
- Figure and table references are meaningful, not decorative.
- Main results are distinguishable from secondary results.
- Exceptions and non-significant results are handled clearly.
- The section avoids discussion-style overinterpretation unless the journal combines Results and Discussion.

## Discussion

- The first paragraph reconnects to the research aim or main finding.
- Results are interpreted rather than repeated.
- The discussion explains how findings relate to prior work.
- Implications are concrete.
- Limitations are specific and connected to interpretation.
- Future work follows logically from the limitations or findings.
- The ending gives a clear take-home message.

## Abstract

- The abstract can stand alone.
- It includes context, aim, method, results, and conclusion/significance.
- The main result is concrete.
- It follows the journal's word limit and structured/unstructured format.
- It does not include unexplained abbreviations unless standard in the field.
- It does not overstate the implications.

## Title

- The title accurately predicts the content.
- It contains important searchable terms.
- It identifies the main object, method, context, or relationship when useful.
- It avoids vague openings such as "A study of" unless conventional.
- It does not claim more than the paper demonstrates.

## Revision Pass Order

1. Confirm scientific meaning and target reader.
2. Fix section-level logic.
3. Fix paragraph order and paragraph functions.
4. Fix sentence order and transitions.
5. Fix grammar, vocabulary, concision, and style.
6. Check title, abstract, and claims against the final paper.
