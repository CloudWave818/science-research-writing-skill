# Section Models

These models are starting points. Adapt them by reverse engineering target articles from the user's field and journal.

## Contents

- Introduction
- Methods
- Results
- Discussion
- Conclusion
- Abstract
- Title

## Introduction

Typical movement: broad territory -> specific problem -> gap or unresolved need -> study aim -> approach or contribution -> optional paper map.

Common functions:

1. Establish the research area and why it matters.
2. Summarize essential current knowledge.
3. Narrow to the specific phenomenon, material, method, population, or question.
4. Identify a limitation, inconsistency, gap, practical need, or methodological problem.
5. Explain why the gap matters.
6. State the aim, hypothesis, research question, or objective.
7. Briefly indicate the approach if it helps the reader enter the paper.
8. Preview the main contribution or novelty without overstating.

Check:

- Does the opening let a non-specialist in the field enter the topic?
- Is the literature doing work, or merely listed?
- Is the gap specific enough to justify this study?
- Does the final paragraph prepare the reader for Methods/Results?

## Methods

Typical movement: design/materials/data -> procedure -> measurements/variables -> analysis -> validation/ethics/reproducibility details.

Common functions:

1. Identify the study design or experimental framework.
2. Describe materials, participants, datasets, instruments, or system setup.
3. Present procedures in the order needed for reproducibility.
4. Justify non-obvious choices, thresholds, parameters, or exclusions.
5. Define outcome measures, variables, or evaluation metrics.
6. Explain statistical, computational, or qualitative analysis.
7. Note ethical approval, availability, robustness checks, or limitations of the method where appropriate.

Check:

- Can a qualified reader understand what was done and why?
- Are chronological order and logical order aligned?
- Are passive forms used to focus on procedure, and active forms used where agency matters?
- Are parameters and sample details complete enough?

## Results

Typical movement: orient to the analysis/figure -> report finding -> quantify or compare -> note exception -> transition to next result.

Common functions:

1. Remind the reader what was tested or measured.
2. Direct attention to a figure, table, model, or comparison.
3. Report the main finding without interpretation overload.
4. Give quantities, direction, magnitude, uncertainty, or significance.
5. Highlight patterns, relationships, categories, or anomalies.
6. Distinguish primary results from secondary or supporting results.
7. Close or transition by preparing the next result.

Check:

- Are results reported before being interpreted?
- Is every figure/table introduced and used?
- Are comparisons explicit?
- Are trends and exceptions both visible?

## Discussion

Typical movement: main finding -> interpretation -> relation to prior work -> contribution/implication -> limitations -> future work or closing message.

Common functions:

1. Reconnect to the research aim or question.
2. Summarize the key finding selectively.
3. Interpret what the finding means.
4. Compare with previous studies: support, extend, contradict, or clarify.
5. Explain mechanisms, causes, or plausible reasons when evidence allows.
6. State theoretical, methodological, clinical, engineering, or practical implications.
7. Acknowledge limitations honestly and specifically.
8. Suggest future work that follows from the study, not generic wishes.
9. End with the contribution or take-home message.

Check:

- Does the Discussion answer the question raised in the Introduction?
- Are claims calibrated to the data?
- Are limitations connected to interpretation?
- Does the ending add value rather than repeat the abstract?

## Conclusion

Typical movement: answer -> contribution -> implication -> optional future direction.

Common functions:

1. State the main answer or conclusion.
2. Identify what the study contributes.
3. Explain why the contribution matters.
4. Point to the next step if it follows naturally.

Check:

- Is it shorter and sharper than the Discussion?
- Does it avoid introducing new evidence?
- Does it avoid simply restating the results?

## Abstract

Typical movement: context/problem -> gap/aim -> methods -> results -> conclusion/significance.

Common functions:

1. Establish the problem or context in one or two sentences.
2. State the aim or research question.
3. Identify the method, data, system, or design.
4. Report the most important results with concrete information.
5. State the main conclusion and significance.

Check:

- Can the abstract stand alone?
- Does it include the actual main result, not just "results are discussed"?
- Is the claim strength appropriate?
- Does it match structured or unstructured journal requirements?

## Title

Useful title functions:

- identify the phenomenon or object studied
- name the method, population, material, system, or context when central
- signal the main relationship, mechanism, or contribution
- avoid unsupported causal claims
- avoid vague words such as "study on" unless the field requires them

Check:

- Does the title predict the paper accurately?
- Are the most searchable terms present?
- Is it shorter than comparable target journal titles unless detail is necessary?
