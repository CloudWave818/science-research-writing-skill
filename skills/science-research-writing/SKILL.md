# Science Research Writing

Use this skill to help researchers turn scientific information into readable, publication-oriented research writing. Prefer structure and meaning before polishing sentences.

## Core Principle

Treat a research paper as a reader-facing narrative, not as a storehouse of information. Every paragraph and sentence should have a clear function that helps the reader understand what the information is doing.

Use a reverse-engineering workflow:

1. Identify the target genre, journal, field, audience, and section.
2. If target articles are available, inspect recent well-written examples from the same field and journal type.
3. Describe what each paragraph or sentence does, not just what it says.
4. Build or adapt a section model from those functions.
5. Draft or revise the user's text so the function, order, language, and strength of claims are clear.
6. Check whether grammar and vocabulary choices support the intended meaning.

## Workflow

### 1. Diagnose Before Rewriting

For existing text, first identify:

- section type and intended journal or discipline
- the main research question, gap, method, finding, and contribution
- paragraph functions and whether their order is logical
- sentences that present information without explaining its function
- overclaiming, underclaiming, ambiguity, repetition that harms clarity, and terminology drift

If the user's goal is urgent polishing, still make small structural fixes when the current structure blocks meaning.

### 2. Use Target Articles When Available

Ask for or use 3-5 recent target articles when the task depends on field-specific conventions. Prefer articles that are:

- from the user's target journal or close journals
- recent, ideally within five years
- close to the user's research design and topic
- clearly written and structurally comparable

Do not copy wording from target articles. Extract transferable functions, ordering, section length, heading conventions, tense patterns, and recurring language moves.

### 3. Build Section Models

Use [section-models.md](references/section-models.md) when drafting or revising a specific paper section.

For each paragraph or sentence, label functions in plain language, such as:

- establishes the research territory
- narrows to the specific problem
- identifies a limitation in prior work
- explains why the method was selected
- reports the main trend
- interprets the finding for the research question
- qualifies the strength of the claim

Then revise so the labels form a coherent path for the reader.

### 4. Choose Language by Function

Use [language-functions.md](references/language-functions.md) for functional phrase patterns. Keep wording field-neutral and adapt it to the user's data.

Prefer:

- consistent technical terms over thesaurus variation
- explicit links between sentences over implicit jumps
- cautious claims when evidence is limited
- active or passive voice according to focus, not habit
- verb tense choices that distinguish established knowledge, methods performed, results found, and claims being advanced

### 5. Apply The Checklist

Use [checklists.md](references/checklists.md) for section review and final pre-submission review.

When returning edits, explain the major function-level changes briefly. If useful, give both:

- a revised version
- a compact note on why the structure works

## Common Task Patterns

### Introduction

Create a narrowing path from broad research territory to specific gap, need, aim, approach, and contribution. Avoid literature review sprawl unless the field expects it.

### Methods

Prioritize reproducibility, rationale, and sequence. Explain method choices when they are not obvious or when they affect interpretation.

### Results

Separate what was found from what it means. Use graphics as part of the narrative and guide the reader to trends, comparisons, and exceptions.

### Discussion

Move from key findings to interpretation, relation to prior work, implications, limitations, and future work. Avoid repeating all results.

### Abstract

Compress the whole narrative: context or problem, gap/aim, approach, main results, significance or implication. Match the journal's abstract type.

### Title

Make the title predict the content accurately. Prefer precise nouns and the main relationship, method, population, or system when relevant.

## Output Style

When editing user text:

- Preserve the user's scientific meaning.
- Flag unclear scientific claims instead of inventing facts.
- Keep changes explainable.
- Provide publication-style English, but avoid ornate prose.
- Do not overuse synonyms for the same concept.
- Make claim strength match evidence.

When the user asks in Chinese, respond in Chinese unless they ask for English-only output. Keep rewritten manuscript passages in the requested manuscript language.
